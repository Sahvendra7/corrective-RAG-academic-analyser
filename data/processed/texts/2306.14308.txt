1. Introduction

In this work, we propose a new prompting method that in-
structs language models to explore diverse reasoning paths
– THOUGHT EXPERIMENTS prompting. Thought experi-
ments are devices of the imagination (Brown & Fehige,
2019).
At its core, a thought experiment uses counter-
factual thinking. A thought experiment imagines differ-
ent (and sometimes hypothetical) scenarios and reasons
through the implications of imagined scenarios, before
summarizing intermediate thoughts into a better under-
standing of the situation in search for the best answer to
the problem. One famous example of a thought experiment
is the trolley problem: should one pull the lever to divert
the runaway trolley onto the side track where one person
would die instead of ﬁve (Thomson, 1984)? By allowing
us to think through “two sides of the coin”, thought ex-
periments have been crucial for humanistic, scientiﬁc, and
artistic pursuits, as well as useful in ﬁguring out complex

As language models gain wide adoption, one important
question in responsible AI is to understand and align the
human values embedded within these models. In particular,
one observation called out in prior research is the poor per-
formance of such models on socially relevant topics such
as morality and law (Hendrycks et al., 2021b).

Answering challenging moral questions requires a partic-
ular type of reasoning – moral reasoning. Moral reason-
ing is reasoning directed towards forming judgments about
what is right or wrong morally (Richardson, 2018). Recent

Moral reasoning and thought experiments go hand in hand.
In this work, we show how thought experiments can be
used to improve moral reasoning, using the Moral Sce-
narios task in MMLU (Multi-task Language Understand-
ing) benchmark (Hendrycks et al., 2021b).
We lay out
recipes for THOUGHT EXPERIMENTS prompting in zero-
shot and few-shot setups, and demonstrate empirically they
help improve this moral reasoning tasks performance. Re-
sults show that our framework elicits counterfactual ques-
tions and answers from the model, which in turn helps im-
prove the accuracy of Moral Scenarios by 9-16% compared
to other zero-shot baselines. We further observed that with
minimal human supervision in the form of 5 few-shot exam-
ples, the accuracy of the task can be improved to as much
as 80%.

Another area of work that we build on is the language mod-
els reasoning literature. Wei et al. (2022) introduces few-
shot chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning, showing impres-
sive performance gains on a variety of tasks, in particu-
lar, on GSM8K benchmark of math word problems. Ko-
jima et al. (2022) shows that instruction-tuned models are
zero-shot reasoners – simply adding “Let’s think step by
step” improves task performance. Self-consistency reason-
ing (Wang et al., 2023) when multiple reasoning paths con-
verge, it is more likely to lead to a correct answer. In a
concurrent work, Tree of Thoughts (ToT) shows that main-
taining a tree of thoughts is powerful in tasks that requires
planning (Yao et al., 2023).

2. Related Work

There are several existing benchmark tasks related to moral-
ity and ethics.
Hendrycks et al. (2021a) introduces the
ETHICS dataset, covering concepts in justice, well-being,
duties, virtues, and commonsense morality.
Multi-task
Language Understanding (MMLU) contains several rele-
vant subtasks, such as Moral Scenarios and Moral Dis-
putes (Hendrycks et al., 2021b). Forbes et al. (2020) in-
troduced the SOCIAL-CHEM-101 dataset, including 104k
source situations and from four subreddits domains. Fol-
low up work builds on datasets such as SOCIAL-CHEM-
101 and ETHICS, and consolidate them into the COM-
MONSENSE NORM BANK, which is used in the Delphi
experiment to train a machine to predict whether a situa-
tion is morally right or wrong (Jiang et al., 2021). Zhao
et al. (2021) introduced the Linguistic Ethical Interventions
(LEI), augmenting question and answer pairs with ethical,
adversarial, and irrelevant interventions. The SCRUPLES
dataset has two subsets (Lourie et al., 2021). The Anec-
dotes subset contains real-life stories with ethical judg-
ments about them. The Dilemmas subset has pairs of ac-
tions and crowdsourced judgments. Finally, the moral per-
missibility task in BIG-bench contains 342 question and
answer pairs drawn from ETHICS and SCRUPLES (Srivas-
tava et al., 2022).

Finally, reasoning can be combined with task decomposi-
tion strategies to allow language models perform complex
tasks (Mishra et al., 2022; Patel et al., 2022; Press et al.,
2022; Khot et al., 2022). For example, Zhou et al. (2023) in-
troduces the least-to-most prompting strategy, showing that
by prompting models to decompose tasks ﬁrst, the model
can solve the decomposed tasks sequentially and then com-
bine the results to achieve better performance. Khot et al.
(2022) proposes a modular and approach for task decompo-
sition, including recursive decomposition of tasks.

Our work aims to bridge the machine ethics and reasoning
literature. We draw from the above mentioned reasoning
and prompting techniques, and focus on leveraging them to
improve moral reasoning.

3. Methods

3.1. Zero-Shot Prompting

We consider four baselines for the zero-shot prompting ap-
proach: direct zero-shot and zero-shot Chain-of-Thought
(CoT) with and without self-consistency. For direct zero-
shot, we ask the model to answer the question with a “Q:
{question} A:” format. For zero-shot CoT, we add “Let’s
think step by step.” to the end of the prompt, which is the
same as (Kojima et al., 2022). For each method, we take the
ﬁrst decoded response from the model as the default answer
(without self-consistency), and the majority voting answer
of ﬁve decoded responses as the self-consistency answer.

While existing work on machine ethics often focus on bi-
nary moral judgements – deciding whether something is
right or wrong, several work has critiqued such simplistic
treatment of morality and proposed a more open-ended gen-
eration task framing (Talat et al., 2021; Bang et al., 2023).
Talat et al. (2021) provides a critique on the Delphi ex-
periment (Jiang et al., 2021), arguing that future work in
machine ethics should “keep actual human moral perspec-
tives and their contradictions ﬁrmly at the forefront”. In
line with the proposal to highlight contradictions in some of

example).

4. Choose. We take multiple decodes from the previous
step, and ask the model to select the best one. This
step is necessary because there are usually multiple
ways of thinking about a situation morally. We pose
this step as a multiple choice question: “Which one of

5. Answer. We present the model chosen summary and

to derive a ﬁnal simple zero-shot answer.

3.2. Few-Shot Prompting

4.2. Task Accuracy

Prior work shows that few-shot learning improves the per-
formance of some tasks, but on other datasets, such as
the ANLI (Adversarial NLI), models still struggle (Brown
et al., 2020). Compared to zero-shot prompting, the few-
shot approach has the limitation that it requires users to cre-
ate hand-crafted examples.

We ﬁrst discuss the zero-shot prompting results (see Ta-
ble 2). The zero-shot out-of-the-box performance of our
model on this task is 57.09% without self-consistency and
53.97% with self-consistency. The zero-shot CoT’s perfor-
mance is worse than direct zero-shot prompting, at 53.18%
and 50.00% respectively without and with self-consistency.
Our proposed zero-shot THOUGHT EXPERIMENTS shows
improvement over the above baselines, at 66.15% and
66.26% without and with self-consistency (+9.06% and
+12.29% over direct zero-shot; +12.97% and +16.26% over
CoT).

We also want to evaluate whether few-shot approaches im-
prove task performance for moral reasoning. We used the
ﬁve examples from the dev split of the dataset as few-shot
exemplars. The authors of the paper manually wrote CoT
and THOUGHT EXPERIMENTS reasoning for each of the
examples. The differences between CoT and thought ex-
periments reasoning demonstration can be subtle. The au-
thors mainly tried to stick to a single reasoning path in
CoT demonstrations, and used more “if” scenarios in the
THOUGHT EXPERIMENTS demonstrations.

At the same time, this work has many limitations. To begin
with, we only conducted experiments on one model and one
task. Future work is needed to understand to what extent
the proposed approach generalizes across different models
and datasets. In addition, future work can also look into
how the ability to conduct thought experiments might differ
with model scale.

Appendix C shows an example from the test set, and its
respective zero-shot CoT and THOUGHT EXPERIMENTS
reasoning chains. We notice that while CoT reasoning of-
ten sticks to the information at hand, THOUGHT EXPERI-

MENTS often poses relevant questions, and provide reason-
able assumptions when the scenarios are under-speciﬁed.
For example, in the “I cut many of the children’s hair last
week” scenario, THOUGHT EXPERIMENTS reasoning asks
questions such as, “What’s the reason for cutting the chil-
dren’s hair?” “Was it justiﬁed?”, and “Were the children
happy with the result?”
As a result of these questions
and answers, THOUGHT EXPERIMENTS reasoning is more
likely to be comprehensive and consider many possibilities,
hence arriving at the correct answer.

Another limitation is the Moral Scenarios task itself. As dis-
cussed in Section 2, treating moral tasks as binary can be
problematic. Although Moral Scenarios tasks aim to focus
only on non-ambiguouscases (Hendrycks et al., 2021a), we
notice in the dataset that some scenarios are under-speciﬁed
and can still be not cleanly “wrong” or “not wrong” as
presented as options in the task. In addition, the Moral
Scenarios task also asks about “moral standards in the US
as of 2020”, which can be limited. Future work should
take these limitations into considerations, and explore open-
ended generation to answer more ambiguous cases, such as
moral dilemmas.
