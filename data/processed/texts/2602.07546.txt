Diffusion Large Language Models (DLLMs) are
inherently ill-suited for variable-length genera-
tion, as their inference is defined on a fixed-length
canvas and implicitly assumes a known target
length. When the length is unknown, as in real-
istic completion and infilling, naively comparing
confidence across mask lengths becomes system-
atically biased, leading to under-generation or
redundant continuations. In this paper, we show
that this failure arises from an intrinsic length-
induced bias in generation confidence estimates,
leaving existing DLLMs without a robust way to
determine generation length and making variable-
length inference unreliable. To address this issue,
we propose LR-DLLM, a length-regularized infer-
ence framework for DLLMs that treats generation
length as an explicit variable and achieves reli-
able length determination at inference time. It
decouples semantic compatibility from length-
induced uncertainty through an explicit length
regularization that corrects biased confidence esti-
mates. Based on this, LR-DLLM enables dynamic
expansion or contraction of the generation span
without modifying the underlying DLLM or its
training procedure. Experiments show that LR-
DLLM achieves 51.3% on HumanEval-
Infilling under fully unknown lengths (+13.4%
vs. DreamOn) and 51.5% average on
four-language McEval (+14.3% vs. DreamOn).

2.1. Preliminary

We study conditional generation problems in which the
output must satisfy a hard context constraint, but the number
of tokens to be generated is not specified. Given an observed
context c, the goal is to generate a token span y ∈V∗of
unknown length and form a completed sequence x = 𝑔(c, y)
that respects the constraint induced by c, where 𝑔(·, ·) is
a deterministic, task-specific composition function. As a
simple example, infilling corresponds to a prefix–suffix
context c = (p, s) with completion x = p∥y∥s. We denote
the (unknown) target span length by 𝐿★:= |y|.

generality while enabling flexible length adaptation.

We propose LR-DLLM, a training-free framework for dy-
namic variable-length decoding in diffusion LLMs that treats
generation length as an explicit inference variable. Rather
than fixing the canvas length a priori, LR-DLLM probes
candidate lengths and leverages the model’s own generation
confidence to search for a more appropriate length. Specifi-
cally, it first evaluates a small set of exponentially spaced
candidate lengths (e.g., 𝐿∈{1, 2, 4, 8, . . . }) and selects the
one maximizing the calibrated confidence as an initial guess.
Starting from this initialization, the framework greedily
expands or contracts the generation span in a bidirectional
manner, guided solely by confidence feedback from the un-
derlying DLLM. The entire procedure operates at inference
time and requires no retraining or architectural modification,
making it directly applicable to existing diffusion backbones.

A key challenge is comparing confidence across different
lengths. Naively selecting the length with the highest average
confidence fails due to an intrinsic length-induced bias in
DLLMs: confidence drifts approximately logarithmically
with canvas length, independent of semantic compatibility.
As a result, raw confidence conflates semantic fit with length-
dependent uncertainty. We address this by introducing a
log 𝐿regularizer that corrects for length bias, yielding a
calibrated confidence–length criterion. The regularization
coefficient is estimated via lightweight probing, enabling
reliable cross-length comparison and robust bidirectional
length adjustment during inference.

3. LR-DLLM

LR-DLLM is a training-free method for variable-length
generation. Unlike standard DLLM, which assumes a fixed
mask length, the target span length under prefix–suffix con-
straints is unknown. LR-DLLM resolves it by modeling
span length as an inference-time variable. It relies on a
length–confidence signal derived from the model’s uncer-
tainty estimates to guide length selection. Using this signal,
LR-DLLM combines a lightweight probing-based initializa-
tion with inference-time refinement for the precise length.

Experiments show that LR-DLLM achieves 51.3%
on HumanEval-Infilling with unknown lengths (+13.4%
over DreamOn) and 51.5% average on the four-
language McEval benchmark (+14.3%), highlighting its
strong generality across models and tasks.

In summary, our key contributions are:

• We identify an intrinsic length-induced bias in DLLM
confidence estimates, demonstrating that naive confi-
dence comparisons across lengths are unreliable for
unknown-length generation.

4. Experiment

LR-DLLM yields consistent improvements across every
backbone, indicating strong model-agnostic generalization.

In this section, we conduct extensive experiments to demon-
strate that LR-DLLM exhibits strong generalization across
models and broad applicability across tasks. Our empirical
study is organized around the following research questions:

On average, LR-DLLM substantially increases mean
across models, despite their very different starting points. For
LLaDA-8B, the mean rises from 23.4 to 36.7, corresponding
to a 56.8% relative improvement; for LLaDA-1.5, it improves
from 31.2 to 36.1, a 15.7% gain. LLaDA-MoE increases
from 35.1 to 40.8, a 16.2% gain, while the Dream-family
models benefit even more: Dream-7B jumps from 22.5 to
44.8, nearly doubling performance with a 99.1% relative
improvement, and DreamCoder-7B increases from 39.1 to
51.3, a 31.2% gain. The fact that LR-DLLM improves both
weaker and stronger backbones suggests that the proposed
inference mechanism provides broadly useful inductive bias
rather than exploiting model-specific quirks.

5.2. Diffusion Large Language Models (DLLMs)

DLLMs conduct iterative denoising with bidirectional con-
text and have recently shown strong performance on general
and code generation. Representative large-scale DLLMs
include the LLADA family (Nie et al., 2025; Zhu et al.,
2025a;b), DEER (Cheng et al., 2025) and Dream (Ye et al.,
2025). However, most DLLM inference pipelines remain
fixed-length, which restricts their applicability to realis-
tic FIM and completion where output length is not given.
Our work complements these DLLMs by enabling variable-
length inference without modifying or retraining the un-
derlying model, making existing DLLMs directly usable in
unknown-length constrained generation.

These trends suggest that Stage II contributes more than
coarse length correction: it actively refines the span interior,
fixing mid-span errors that would otherwise persist even
when the overall length is plausible. As a result, LR-DLLM
achieves markedly better Multi-line coherence, indicating
that mid-span refinement is a crucial complement to Stage I’s
boundary-driven length adjustment.

6. Conclusion

We presented LR-DLLM, a training-free framework for
unknown-length constrained generation in diffusion LLMs.
By treating length as an explicit inference variable and op-
timizing a debiased length–confidence signal, LR-DLLM
supports bidirectional length adjustment (expansion and con-
traction) without modifying the base model. Beyond infill-
ing, we evaluate LR-DLLM on general-purpose benchmarks
(MATH500, GSM8K, and HumanEval), demonstrating that
it also improves standard left-to-right generation.

A. Experimental Setup and Details

Baseline availability and coverage.
We compare against representative variable-length diffusion baselines when their
public releases allow reproducible evaluation under our settings. However, some contemporary methods cannot be uniformly
evaluated across backbones due to limitations in released checkpoints and implementation availability. DreamOn (Wu et al.,
2025) is only publicly released with DreamCoder checkpoints, and training the full system from scratch is computationally
expensive (e.g., requiring large-scale multi-GPU training as reported by the authors); therefore, we report DreamOn
comparisons only on the DreamCoder backbone. FlexMDM (Kim et al., 2025) does not provide public code at the time
of writing; we thus cite the numbers reported in the original paper, noting that the paper reports results only for a limited
subset of settings. Finally, DAEDAL (Li et al., 2025) is designed around the LLaDA family and is not directly applicable to
Dream under its current framework; hence, we include DAEDAL only in the settings where it is compatible. Across all
reported comparisons, we align inference budgets and evaluation protocols as closely as possible to ensure fairness, and we
explicitly mark settings that are not applicable or not available.

Decoding and inference configuration.
Unless otherwise specified, all experiments use the same decoding configuration
for fair comparison. Specifically, we apply standard LLM sampling with nucleus sampling probability 𝑝=0.9, no top-𝑘
truncation, and temperature 0.2 across all benchmarks. The same decoding settings are used for LR-DLLM and all baseline
methods whenever applicable. All reported results are obtained under fixed random seeds and deterministic decoding
settings. We observed negligible variance across repeated runs (<0.5 on HumanEval-Infilling).

B. Run-to-Run Variability Analysis

Across all models and infilling settings, the run-to-run variability of LR-DLLM remains consistently small. As shown in
Table 7, the standard deviation of is below 0.5 in all cases, indicating that the observed performance gains are
not driven by stochastic fluctuations across runs. This stability holds across heterogeneous backbones and span regimes,

In particular, the magnitude of variability is an order of magnitude smaller than the absolute improvements reported in the
main results, confirming that the benefits of LR-DLLM are stable rather than within noise.

C. Clarification on Task-Agnosticity

In this paper, the term task-agnostic refers to the structural generality of the proposed inference framework, rather than
empirical validation across all possible task categories. Specifically, LR-DLLM operates on a generic prefix–suffix
composition x = p∥y∥s and does not rely on task-specific supervision, loss functions, or architectural modifications.

In practice, this formulation subsumes two broad and commonly used generation paradigms: (i) prefix–suffix infilling (FIM),
where both p and s are non-empty, and (ii) left-to-right continuation, which is recovered as the special case s = ∅. Our
empirical evaluation focuses on representative tasks within these paradigms, including code completion, multi-line infilling,
and mathematical reasoning, which naturally instantiate the above structure.

We do not claim that LR-DLLM has been empirically validated on all task families (e.g., dialogue, summarization, or
instruction following). Rather, the task-agnostic claim is intended in the sense that the method is not specialized to any
particular task, and can in principle be applied to any generation problem that can be cast into a prefix–suffix composition
with unknown span length.

Setup.
We evaluate fixed-length infilling on HumanEval-Infilling using the DreamCoder backbone by sweeping the mask
length 𝐿∈{4, 8, 16, 32, 64} under identical inference budgets and decoding settings. accuracy is reported separately
for Single-Line and Multi-Line infilling. Table 8 summarizes the numerical results, and Figure 4 visualizes the performance
trends.

Results.
As shown in Table 8, fixed-length performance exhibits a strong dependence on the chosen mask length. For

While ground-truth span lengths are available for HumanEval-Infilling, we focus on downstream correctness as the primary
criterion, since multiple lengths may admit semantically valid solutions. Direct length error metrics (e.g., MAE) are therefore
not fully aligned with task success.

E. Length-Induced Entropy in FIM and Continuation

In both fill-in-the-middle (FIM) and standard left-to-right continuation, we consistently observe a systematic trend: as the
“free” generation region becomes longer, token predictions become less certain, and uncertainty tends to increase as tokens
move farther away from the anchoring context (i.e., the boundary between conditioned and unconditioned spans). This
phenomenon suggests that measured uncertainty contains an intrinsic length-dependent component in addition to the model’s
quality-dependent component.

A statistical-physics view.
We model this effect using the classical relation between entropy and the number of admissible
microstates:
𝑆= 𝑘log 𝑁,

G. Forward-Call Efficiency Distribution Across Models

We quantify the inference-time overhead of LR-DLLM using the ratio Forward Calls/Generated Tokens, where
Forward Calls counts the number of model invocations used to evaluate AVGconf(𝐿) (and thus CL(𝐿)) during Stage I
probing and Stage II local length search, and Generated Tokens is the number of committed tokens produced by
Stage II. Figure 5 visualizes the per-instance distribution of this ratio for three backbones (Dream, DreamCoder, and
LLaDA-8B) under an identical plotting protocol, while Table 9 reports summary statistics.

Across all three models, the distributions are markedly right-skewed: the median is consistently lower than the mean (e.g.,
Dream: 3.43 vs. 3.66; DreamCoder: 4.00 vs. 4.89; LLaDA-8B: 4.63 vs. 5.12), indicating that most instances incur a stable
and moderate overhead, while a small number of hard cases forms a long tail and inflates the mean. Using the median as a
robust measure of typical behavior, Dream is the most efficient backbone (median 3.43), followed by DreamCoder (median
4.00) and LLaDA-8B (median 4.63). The tails differ more substantially: Dream exhibits the tightest distribution (P95 = 5.00),
whereas DreamCoder shows the heaviest tail (P95 = 11.23), with LLaDA-8B in between (P95 = 8.00). All three models have
rare extreme outliers (max around 28–33), which are far beyond their respective P95 values, suggesting that they occur
infrequently but can arise when many local length updates are triggered for instances with short effective generations or
unstable local optima.

Overall, these results corroborate that, despite a conservative worst-case bound, LR-DLLM typically converges in only a few
local updates per committed token on standard benchmarks, leading to a modest overhead for the vast majority of instances.

H. Additional Comparison with dLLM-Var

We include an auxiliary comparison with dLLM-Var, a representative training-based variable-length method for diffusion
LLMs. We do not report it in the main tables because its current public release is limited to a single trained checkpoint
(LLADA-8B-Instruct) and a specific inference variant (block diffusion), and it only supports one-sided (backward-only)
length growth. These constraints prevent a consistent evaluation across the multiple backbones and general prefix–suffix
FIM settings considered in this paper. Nevertheless, for completeness, we report results on the subset of benchmarks that
dLLM-Var can be evaluated on. For a fair comparison, we use the same maximum probing length MAX_LENGTH = 256 for
LR-DLLM in all experiments below.

Discussion.
Table 10 shows that LR-DLLM consistently outperforms dLLM-Var on the supported subset. Concretely,
LR-DLLM improves GSM8K accuracy from 70.9 to 75.1 (+4.2 points), more than doubles performance on MATH500 from
18.4 to 39.8 (+21.4 points), and boosts HumanEval from 17.7 to 31.7 (+14.0 points). These gains are achieved
while remaining training-free and model-agnostic, whereas dLLM-Var is tied to a specific trained LLADA-8B-Instruct
checkpoint and a block-diffusion procedure with one-sided length growth. More importantly, LR-DLLM supports bidirectional
length adjustment (both expansion and contraction) under general constrained generation, which is essential for prefix–suffix
FIM and settings with large length variance. A broader open-source release of dLLM-Var covering more backbones and
more general constrained generation settings would enable a more comprehensive comparison in future work.

I. Other Related Work

Several recent works have explored diffusion-based language models (DLLMs) from complementary perspectives. Ul-
traLLaDA (He et al., 2025) focuses on extending the context window of DLLMs to very long sequences (up to 128K
tokens), addressing scalability in context length rather than the inference-time determination of unknown span length.
Diffusion-LM (Li et al., 2022) and S3 (Xiong et al., 2025) demonstrate that diffusion-based formulations can be advantageous
for conditional generation, but they operate under fixed-length generation assumptions and do not address variable-length
inference.

Other works investigate efficiency and decoding acceleration for DLLMs. DPad (Chen et al., 2025) and Sparse-dLLM (Song
et al., 2025) study architectural and systems-level optimizations, such as attention sparsification and KV-cache management,
to improve inference speed. These methods are orthogonal to our focus, as they assume a predetermined generation length
and do not consider length selection as part of the inference process.
